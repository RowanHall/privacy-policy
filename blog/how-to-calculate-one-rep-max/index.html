How to calculate your one-rep max

Updated July 2026 · 6 min read

Your one-rep max (1RM) is the most weight you can lift for a single rep of an exercise. It's the reference point almost every strength program is built on — but you don't need to actually attempt a true max to know it. With a formula, you can estimate your 1RM from any hard set, safely and in seconds.

Why estimate instead of testing a true max

Testing an actual 1RM is fatiguing, technically risky, and only worthwhile now and then. Estimating it from a normal working set lets you:

  • Track strength progress week to week without maxing out
  • Pick the right training weights from percentages
  • Compare lifts and spot new personal records automatically

The one-rep max formulas

Every 1RM estimate is based on a simple idea: the more reps you can do with a given weight, the lighter that weight is relative to your max. The two most trusted formulas are Epley and Brzycki.

Epley formula

1RM = weight × (1 + reps ÷ 30)

So a 5-rep set at 225 lb estimates to 225 × (1 + 5 ÷ 30) ≈ 262 lb.

Brzycki formula

1RM = weight × 36 ÷ (37 − reps)

The same set gives 225 × 36 ÷ (37 − 5) ≈ 253 lb.

Which formula should you use?

No single formula is "correct" — they're models, and each carries a small bias. Epley tends to read slightly higher at moderate reps; Brzycki slightly lower. The most robust approach, and what our calculator does, is to average several formulas (Epley, Brzycki and Lombardi) to smooth out their individual quirks.

Accuracy drops as reps climb. Estimates are most reliable at 10 reps or fewer, taken close to failure. A set of 20 with reps left in the tank will overestimate your max.

Turn your 1RM into a training plan

Once you have an estimated 1RM, most programs prescribe work as a percentage of it. A rough guide:

Max strength85–95% of 1RM2–5
Strength & size75–85% of 1RM5–8
Hypertrophy65–75% of 1RM8–12

Frequently asked questions

How accurate are one-rep max calculators?

For sets of 1–10 reps taken close to failure, estimates are typically within a few percent of a tested max — accurate enough to program from. Higher-rep sets and sets far from failure are less reliable.

Can I calculate 1RM from a set of 10?

Yes. Ten reps is right at the edge of the reliable range. If you can, use a heavier set of 3–8 reps for a tighter estimate.

Does one-rep max transfer between exercises?

No — your 1RM is specific to each lift. Track squat, bench and deadlift separately, which is exactly how KovaFit stores them.
